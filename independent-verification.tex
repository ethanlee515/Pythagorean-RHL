\section{Independent Verification}
\label{sec:independent-verification}

The formal theorem is useful only if its statement is the theorem one intended
to prove.
For this reason, one should distinguish the size of the artifact from the part
that a human reader needs to audit.
The development has three main technical layers: a probability layer for KL,
Pinsker, Pythagorean preservation, and discrete Gaussians; a program-logic
layer for additive-error and Pythagorean reasoning over SSProve programs; and
an LMSS/noise-flooding layer that encodes the games, reduction, metric
interface, and final security proof.
A reader must trust that the formal statement faithfully represents the
cryptographic objects, that the probability and program-logic theorems have
the advertised mathematical content, and that Rocq has checked the proof of
the final theorem from SSProve's semantics and the listed assumptions.

\subsection{Trusted Computing Base}
\label{sec:tcb}

The trusted computing base is the usual one for a Rocq/SSProve development,
plus the mathematical libraries needed to talk about countable probability.
At the bottom is the Rocq kernel and standard library infrastructure,
including the construction of real numbers, classical reasoning principles used
by the imported libraries, and the typeclass/canonical-structure machinery on
which the scripts rely.
MathComp and MathComp Analysis provide the algebraic hierarchy, choice types,
finite tuples, real analysis, infinite sums, summability, logarithms,
exponentials, and the distribution interface used for KL divergence and
discrete Gaussians.
SSProve provides the concrete syntax and semantics of probabilistic programs,
packages, oracle linking, heaps, subdistributions, and cryptographic games.

If a reader does not accept Rocq, MathComp/Analysis, or SSProve as a
foundation, this paper cannot remove that concern; many textbooks' worth of
mathematics and programming-language semantics live below our development.
Our claim starts above that foundation.
Given SSProve's semantics for pseudocode and games, and MathComp's semantics
for distributions and real analysis, the formal artifact checks that the
noise-flooding theorem follows from the stated scheme, correctness, metric,
and IND-CPA assumptions.
The program-logic rules, trace compiler, KL lemmas, and game hops are not part
of the TCB: they are Rocq theorems proved from those underlying semantics.
Once the definitions are accepted, the remaining claim is deliberately simple:
Rocq typechecks the proof.
The command
\[
  \texttt{Print Assumptions Secure.is\_secure}
\]
is the machine-checkable summary of what remains outside the proof: the
scheme/correctness/metric interfaces, the assumed IND-CPA bound, positivity of
the Gaussian-width multiplier, and the ordinary library/classical assumptions
inherited from Rocq, MathComp, MathComp Analysis, and SSProve.

The final-theorem excerpt in \Cref{app:final-reduction-theorem} is intentionally

The full development is about 35,000 lines, with roughly 14,000 lines in the
split noise-flooding security proof alone.
The artifact is the appropriate medium for that part.

The assumptions that should receive the most cryptographic attention are the
following.
The IND-CPA assumption in \texttt{Indcpa.v} is the standard black-box security
hypothesis for the underlying scheme, stated against the SSProve IND-CPA game.
The IND-CPA-D game in \texttt{Indcpad.v} is not assumed secure; it is the game
proved secure after applying noise flooding.
The final theorem currently uses the support-level perfect-correctness
interface in \texttt{ApproxFHE.v}; approximate correctness with explicit
correctness-error accounting is kept as a separate interface.
The metric interface in \texttt{ApproxFHE.v} assumes origin-centered integer
charts:
\[
  I_a(a)=0,\qquad
  d(a,b)=\lVert I_a(b)\rVert,\qquad
  I_b^{-1}(x)=I_a^{-1}(x+I_a(b)).
\]
These are the assumptions connecting the abstract message metric to the
integer-vector Gaussian analysis.
Finally, the noise-flooding parameter module assumes only that the Gaussian
width multiplier is positive.

\subsection{Probability and Discrete-Gaussian Review}
\label{sec:probability-review}

The probability layer is the part of the artifact that a mathematically minded
reader should audit as ordinary analysis.
The relevant human-facing statements are the definitions of KL divergence and
finite KL, Pinsker's inequality for MathComp distributions, the conditional
coordinate chain bound used for Pythagorean preservation, and the
equal-variance KL formula for discrete Gaussians over the integers.
These are stated in \Cref{sec:verified-kl-analysis}; their Rocq proofs live
primarily in \texttt{Probability/KL/} and
\texttt{Probability/DiscreteGaussians/}.

As with the program logic, the full proof scripts are not the intended reading
order.
The paper should give pen-and-paper proofs of the mathematical facts that are
not immediate, especially the chain-rule/summability argument and the
discrete-Gaussian KL calculation.
The formal development then checks the details that are easy to lose in an
informal proof over countable distributions: absolute continuity,
positive/negative-part summability, interchange of countable sums, and the
normalization and moment facts for the integer Gaussian.

This part of the project also produced some library-adjacent ``community
service'' for MathComp Analysis.
The local \texttt{RealSumExtras.v} file factors out reusable facts about
MathComp's experimental \texttt{sum}/\texttt{summable} interface, including the
proved Fubini-style interchange lemma
\[
  \texttt{interchange\_psum\_proved}.
\]
Those facts were developed while discharging the summability obligations for
the KL analysis.
A version of this material was also submitted upstream to MathComp Analysis,
where it helps reduce reliance on admitted real-summation infrastructure.

\subsection{Program-Logic Review}
\label{sec:program-logic-review}

For a PL or verification reader, the audit story is the standard soundness
story.
The development defines semantic judgments for support-style Hoare reasoning,
completed-output additive error, and Pythagorean KL bookkeeping.
Each inference rule is a theorem about those semantic definitions.
A human reader should not be expected to step through the corresponding Rocq
proof scripts line by line.
The human-facing artifact is the selected collection of semantic definitions
and rule statements, together with mathematical explanations for the few rules
whose soundness is not immediate.

The rule statements themselves are compact.
\Cref{sec:program-logic} displays the core rules used by the security proof:
completed additive-error rules, Pythagorean sampling and sequencing rules, the
Micciancio--Walter bridge, and the trace-compiler replacement principles.
Even conservatively counting around twenty externally meaningful rules at ten
to twenty lines each, the surface to inspect is on the order of a few hundred
lines, not thousands of lines of proof script.

The proof scripts remain important, but as the machine-checking substrate.
For routine rules, soundness is a direct unfolding of the semantic judgment
plus standard distribution facts.
For the nontrivial rules---completed-output sequencing, the trace compiler,
and the Pythagorean final-marginal theorem behind the Micciancio--Walter
bridge---the paper should provide pen-and-paper arguments at the level of
mathematics and proof structure.
The Rocq proofs then check that those arguments have no hidden side conditions.

For cryptographers, this part should look familiar at a high level.
We define judgments by their intended semantics, prove inference rules sound,
and then use those rules to structure the game-hopping proof.
The unusual feature is not the soundness methodology itself, but the
Pythagorean bookkeeping: instead of composing statistical-distance errors
linearly at each program step, the proof records conditional KL costs and pays
the square-root loss once at the end.
