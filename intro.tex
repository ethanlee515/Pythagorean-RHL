\section{Introduction}

In program analysis, pairs of programs are often compared with each other.
For example, we may show that an optimized program implements its specification faithfully, or is equivalent to a reference implementation.
Cryptography researchers often also prove that certain sub-protocols are indistinguishable from idealized building blocks that are (by definition) composable and secure.
Machinery for these analyses falls under the \emph{relational Hoare Logic} (RHL) framework.
In this work, we present a variant of RHL that includes \emph{Pythagorean probability preservation} in lattice cryptography.

The term \emph{Pythagorean probability preservation} is coined by theory cryptography work \cite{mw17}, which generalizes an analysis \cite{kl-queries} involving Kullback-Leibler divergence.
Today, Pythagorean probability preservation is used to argue the security of the ``CKKS" fully homomorphic encryption, as well as to bound the security loss due to floating-point precision limitations.
Roughly speaking, suppose two distributions $\mathcal{D}$ and $\mathcal{D}'$ are $\varepsilon$-close, and a program $c$ takes $n$ samples from either $\mathcal{D}$ or $\mathcal{D}'$ before outputting $y$ or $y'$ respectively.
By \emph{data processing inequality} or \emph{up-to-bad arguments} then, we would conclude that $y$ and $y'$ are $(n\cdot\varepsilon)$-close.
This standard analysis would however make use of only that $\mathcal{D}$ and $\mathcal{D}'$ are close in total variation distance.
On the other hand, Pythagorean probability preservation achieves a sharper $O(\sqrt{n})$ bound instead, in the cases where $\mathcal{D}$ and $\mathcal{D}'$ are additionally also close in KL-divergence.

The Pythagorean probability preservation property is a cryptographic one;
in terms of programming languages, it corresponds to analysis at the \emph{semantics} level.
Much like data-processing inequality is packaged in program logic terms using RHL and up-to-bad arguments then, we present \emph{Relational Hoare Logic with Pythagorean Errors} in order to apply Pythagorean probability preservation directly at the \emph{syntactic} level.
We compare our logic with other existing logics for cryptography and differential privacy in \Cref{sec:related-works}.
The accompanying Rocq formalization is open source at \url{https://github.com/ethanlee515/mending}.

Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE) enables arbitrary computation on encrypted data, ensuring that privacy is preserved even during processing, and making it a key enabler of secure cloud analytics, outsourced ML, and multiparty computation. With recent advances reducing the cost of homomorphic operations, FHE is becoming increasingly practical for real-world confidential computation.

This technique, known as noise flooding, is especially useful in settings where multiple parties jointly process encrypted data, as in \emph{threshold FHE}, ensuring that no intermediate information is revealed to participants. Similar noise-management techniques also appear in other cryptographic and privacy-preserving contexts, such as floating-point Gaussian sampling, highlighting their broader importance for safe and reliable computation on sensitive data.
