\section{Comparison with Other Program Logics}
\label{sec:related-works}

We first recall how standard hybrid arguments are typically formalized in RHL and compare it to our additive errors logic.
We then compare our full logic to apRHL \cite{aprhl}.

\subsection{Additive Errors and Up-to-bad Arguments}
\label{sec:up-to-bad-event}

\emph{Up-to-bad argument} is another name for what cryptography researchers often refer to as the data-processing inequality, union bound, or hybrid arguments.
In toolchains such as EasyCrypt,
they are usually treated using a combination of relational and probabilistic Hoare logic.
More concretely, in RHL we define a \emph{bad event} $B$ and set the post-condition to the form $\neg B \implies \Psi$, and in pHL we show the probability of $B$ is low.
These existing toolchains then provide specialized inference rules taking two judgments of the form above and automatically concluding that two programs' outputs are $\varepsilon$-close.
Our additive errors judgment can be seen as simply combining the two judgments above,
with an additional advantage of no longer requiring an explicit description of the ``bad event" $B$.
More precisely, if needed, we can connect the two different styles as follows:
\begin{mathpar}
\inferrule[UtB-Hyb]
    {\vdash c_1 \sim c_2:\Phi\implies(\neg B_2 \Rightarrow\Psi) \\ \vdash \Pr[c_2:\Phi\implies B_2]<\varepsilon}
  {\vdash c_1 \sim_\varepsilon c_2:\Phi\implies\Psi}
\end{mathpar}
where $B_2$ is a predicate on the second program's final memory.

\subsection{Comparison to apRHL}

Approximate relational Hoare logic \cite{aprhl} is another logic for analysis involving KL divergence.
The main motivation is to analyze differential privacy bounds of the form
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dp-generic-bound}
\Pr[E_1] \leq e^\varepsilon \Pr[E_2] + \delta.
\end{equation}
\newcommand{\aprhl}{\mathsf{ap}}
Accordingly, it is based on relational triples of the form:
$$\vDash\aprhl \set{\Phi}c_1\sim_{\langle\varepsilon, \delta\rangle} c_2:\set{\Psi}$$
Roughly speaking, this judgment states that for all initial memories $(m_1, m_2)$, there exists \emph{two} couplings $\cP_L$ and $\cP_R$ such that:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The two couplings are close, with a \emph{multiplicative privacy budget} factor on top of the total variation distance:
    \[
        \sup_E \abs{\Pr_{m_1\gets \cP_L}[E(m_1)] -  e^\varepsilon \Pr_{m_2\gets \cP_R}[E(m_2)]}\leq \delta
    \]
    \item The first marginal of $\cP_L$ matches $\llbracket c_1\rrbracket (m_1)$.
    \item The second marginal of $\cP_R$ matches $\llbracket c_2\rrbracket (m_2)$.
\end{itemize}

Now, in many popular differential privacy settings (e.g. database queries), one aims to prove bounds in the form of \Cref{eq:dp-generic-bound}.
In the game-based cryptographic primitive (e.g. encryption; IND-CPA and families) setting however, one rarely considers this multiplicative lax.
To make use of \Cref{eq:dp-generic-bound} then, unfortunately it seems like we must use the loose bounds
$e^\varepsilon\approx1+\varepsilon$ and $\Pr[E_2]\leq 1$ to arrive at
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:aprhl-to-hybrid-bound}
    \abs{\Pr[E_1]-\Pr[E_2]}\lesssim\delta+\varepsilon
\end{equation}
In other words, the $\varepsilon$ term might not offer much more than what $\delta$ already does for analyzing primitives.

Now, apRHL admits the following sequence rule:
\begin{mathpar}
\inferrule[ApRHL-Seq]{
    c_i:X_i\rightarrow\code X_i' \\
    c_i':X_i'\rightarrow Y_i \\
    \vDash\aprhl\set{P}c_1\sim_{\langle \varepsilon, \delta\rangle}c_2\set{R}\\
    \vDash\aprhl\set{R}c_1'\sim_{\langle\varepsilon', \delta'\rangle}c_2'\set{Q}
}{
    \vDash\aprhl\set{P}c_1;c_1'\sim_{\langle\varepsilon+\varepsilon', \delta+\delta'\rangle}c_2;c_2'\set{Q}
}
\end{mathpar}
In particular, the parameters $\delta$ and $\varepsilon$ both grow linearly in the number of applications of the sequence rule.
That is, the error grows linearly in the length of the experiment, and therefore in the number of queries the adversary is allowed in a cryptographic game.
There are no sub-linear scalings here that we would achieve by Pythagorean probability preservation.

Finally, if we set $\varepsilon=0$ in ApRHL, we claim that our judgment is strictly more expressive than apRHL, due to our different way of handling couplings and attributing errors.
In particular, apRHL judgments always implies our hybrid judgment, but not the other way around.
This can also be interpreted as a form of interoperability:
apRHL judgments with $\varepsilon=0$ can be converted to our judgment.

    $$\vDash\UTB \set{\Phi} c_1\sim_\delta c_2\set{\Psi}$$
