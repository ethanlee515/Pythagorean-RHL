\subsection{Goals and Theorem Statements}

For the sake of completeness,
we sketch the \emph{noise flooding} \cite{lmss} countermeasure to the LM attack \cite{lm} against the CKKS \cite{ckks} encryption scheme.
Along the way, we outline two design choices we have made in \Cref{sec:isometry} and \Cref{sec:query-bound}.
Beside these, we refer the interested reader to the literature for cryptographic intuitions.

\subsubsection{On Locally Euclidean Metric Spaces}
\label{sec:isometry}

In lattice cryptography, one often considers polynomials and integers modulo some ideal generated by some polynomial $x^n+1$ and some number $q\in\bbN$.
Instead, we generalize the analysis to any locally-$\bbZ^n$ metric space.

We recall the textbook definition of a metric space in \Cref{def:metric-space}.
We now generalize the \emph{approximate correctness} notion for FHEs to metric spaces:
\begin{definition}
    Let $\varepsilon>0$ and $\delta>0$ be fixed parameters.
    Let $(X, d)$ be a metric space.
    A HE scheme $S$ is $(\delta, \varepsilon)$-\emph{approximately correct} if for all valid keys $(pk, evk, sk)\in\supp(keygen)$ and valid functions $f:X^*\rightarrow X$ and input $m\in X^*$,
    $$\Pr[d(f(m), (Dec\circ Eval_f\circ Enc)(m))\geq \delta]\leq \varepsilon$$
    where the probability is taken over randomness of the encryption, evaluation, and decryption algorithms.
\end{definition}

In general FHEs such as CKKS, we have $\varepsilon>0$ and $\delta>0$ for FHEs.
For simplicity, we verify the $\varepsilon=0$ case.
Finally, we need to connect discrete Gaussian distributions to this metric space setting.
As discrete Gaussian distributions are well-defined on $\bbZ^n$, we simply require a local isometry between $\bbZ^n$ with our metric space as defined by \Cref{def:local-isometry}.
We remark that the isometry indeed needs to be \emph{local}, as there is no global isometry between $\bbZ$ and the cyclic group $\bbZ/q\bbZ$ of lattice cryptography.

\subsubsection{Treatment of Query-Bounded Attackers}
\label{sec:query-bound}

Attackers against cryptographic constructions are often modeled as oracle algorithms.
Intuitively, an attacker against an encryption scheme aims to decrypt some target ciphertexts without knowing the secret decryption key;
such an adversary would be given an encryption oracle that generates target ciphertexts.

In the noise flooding analysis, as we will see in \Cref{sec:games}, the adversary is allowed to make at most $q\in\bbN$ queries to an oracle.
An earlier version of the development assumed the attacker was factorized into $q$ subroutines, one corresponding to each oracle query.
The current trace-compilation formalism described in the previous section is intended to remove this as a semantic assumption for concrete SSProve adversaries: one can compile the first $q$ calls to a selected operation identifier and reason about the resulting code.
In the Coq development, this is paired with an explicit decryption-query counter in both the real and simulated oracles.
Calls beyond the bound assert-fail on both sides, so compiling the first $q$ calls to the decryption identifier is enough to cover every non-failing decryption response.
The games below therefore use a factored presentation only as notation for the code produced by the trace compiler:
the adversary in the formal development is still an ordinary SSProve package, and the separate query routines should be read as the continuation pieces exposed by $\operatorname{compileCalls}$.
There remains a smaller limitation of our current Pythagorean judgment:
For example, consider the two procedures in \Cref{alg:sample-with-half-prob}.
There is no consistent way of writing down a Pythagorean judgment relating these two programs, as our judgment is parameterized by a fixed number of sampling operations,
and does not capture the case where the number of samples itself is also random.

\begin{algorithm}
    \caption{Limitation of our program logic}
    \label{alg:sample-with-half-prob}
    \begin{algorithmic}
        \Procedure{Left}{}
        \State $b\gets\set{0, 1}$
        \If{$b$}
        \State $x\gets D_x$
        \EndIf
        \EndProcedure
        \Procedure{Right}{}
        \State $b\gets\set{0, 1}$
        \If{$b$}
        \State $x\gets D_x'$
        \EndIf
        \EndProcedure
    \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

On pen-and-paper proofs, this is handled implicitly by a generalization of what the Easycrypt community refers to as \emph{reflection} \cite{reflection}.
That is, all code snippets and procedures are synonymous and interchangeable with their respective output distributions.
For instance, Pythagorean probability preservation (\Cref{def:pythagorean-probability-preservation}) is applied directly to the distribution of the entire transcript of the protocol as if the adversary is already a distribution over its oracle query inputs and its eventual response to the challenger.

In existing literature,
this issue is typically handled using \emph{call invariants} and \emph{adversarial call rules} in the union bound context.
For call invariants, one intuitively write down a sub-coupling that holds up to some \emph{bad event};
for example, given two distributions $D_x$ and $D_{x'}$ such that $\Delta(D_x, D_{x'})<\varepsilon$,
one can write down a coupling $D_{x, x'}$ so that $\Pr[x=x']=1-\varepsilon$.
In our KL divergence settings, however, it is unclear how to generalize this coupling machinery.
As for adversarial call rules,
thematically they do not fit in the foundational setting of SSProve.
In Easycrypt, adversarial call rules are necessary as adversaries are opaque objects that one cannot interact with except through adversarial call rules.
As the adversaries are concrete programs in SSProve, to the best of our knowledge, there has not been any attempts in implementing any Easycrypt-style adversarial call rules in SSProve.
If we were to write down an adversarial call rule for our setting, this would only be pushing these factorization challenges one layer down,
where now the soundness of the rule needs to be verified.

For the general oracle setting,
our current strategy is precisely to construct this factorization as code and verify its correctness.
The trace machinery steps through the SSProve syntax to read out the first $q$ selected oracle queries, records enough of the surrounding computation to resume the program, and proves that linking the compiled program with the same package preserves the original semantics.
One small wrinkle, invisible in the usual pen-and-paper presentation, is that the suspended trace contains packed values.
When replaying a trace against the original program, a malformed packed value may fail to decode at the type expected by the next command.
The compiler represents this impossible replay path by an explicitly null program rather than by a syntactic equality of continuations.
Thus the key rebase property for compiled traces is semantic: invalid replay branches contribute the null distribution, even if the raw code following that branch is not syntactically the same on the two sides.
This is why the formal proof distinguishes ordinary program equalities from distributional equalities of the resulting \texttt{Pr\_code} semantics.
In the Coq development, the query-counting part is reflected in the concrete games by the decryption counter, and the downstream conversion from an $\AEJ$ judgment on completed game outputs to a total-variation bound on the sampled winning bit has been formalized.
The simulator Gaussian has also been bridged to the real tuple Gaussian: mapping the SSProve vector sample through \texttt{toIntVec} gives exactly \texttt{n\_dg}.
The current concrete loss is
\[
  2\sqrt{\frac{q\cdot n/(2\gamma^2)}{2}},
\]
where $n$ is the metric dimension and $\gamma$ is the Gaussian-width multiplier.
The scalar and per-coordinate arithmetic budget is proved formally: if a coordinate shift is bounded by the ciphertext error bound, then its one-dimensional Gaussian KL cost is at most $1/(2\gamma^2)$, and max-norm vector distance gives this premise for every coordinate.
The corresponding product-level Pythagorean fact for shifted tuple Gaussians is also proved.

The formalization packages the real and simulated successful decryption samples as marginals of shifted tuple Gaussians.
This exposes one geometric obligation hidden in the older proof sketch: the real oracle postprocesses through \texttt{inverse\_isometry} centered at \texttt{dec' sk c}, while the simulator postprocesses through \texttt{inverse\_isometry} centered at the table plaintext.
The current metric interface gives local isometry facts inside a fixed chart, but not the chart-change/data-processing theorem saying that these two message-space Gaussian pushforwards are KL-close when the centers are close.
This missing principle is named in Coq as \texttt{noise\_flooding\_message\_gaussian\_kl}.
Assuming it, the development proves the successful-decrypt one-call Pythagorean budget, including the raw-code rule needed by the oracle-body proof.
The exact-center chart case, where the two centers agree, is already axiom-free.

The first decrypt-replacement hybrid layer is present in Coq.
The closed package \texttt{IndCpadSimDecryptOracle} copies the real IND-CPAD encrypt/eval code and changes only \texttt{oracle\_decrypt} to the simulator's plaintext/noise response.
The compile bridge says that, under the one-call decrypt package judgment and the heap invariant, the compiled real game and the compiled decrypt-hybrid game are related by additive error $\sqrt{q\epsilon/2}$, with $\epsilon=n/(2\gamma^2)$.
The concrete one-call decrypt judgment itself is now proved after factoring the deterministic decrypt prefix from the noisy continuation.
The initialized challenger is factored so that \texttt{compileRule} is applied only to the moved adversary's final \texttt{guess} body.
The final theorem is assembled from named bridges between this compiled decrypt-hybrid game, the self-linked simulator game, and the existing IND-CPA reduction presentation.
The remaining formal endpoint work is to prove the two zero-error identifications at the end of this chain.

\subsubsection{Security Games}
\label{sec:games}

Now we state the main lemma that we want to formally verify.
We aim to show that the two experiments (or \emph{cryptographic games}) given in \Cref{alg:indcpa-games} are indistinguishable.
That is, as the FHE scheme is approximately correct, the adversary cannot distinguish between any homomorphically evaluated computation and unencrypted computation.
This is relevant in the FHE setting, as the noises and errors could leak information otherwise, which was indeed the principle behind the LM attack.
More formally, we aim to prove the following theorem:
\begin{theorem}
    \label{thm:noise-flooding-secure}
    Let $\epsilon_{\mathsf{nf}}=n/(2\gamma^2)$, where $n$ is the dimension of the local Gaussian chart and $\gamma$ is the Gaussian-width multiplier.
    Up to the IND-CPA security of the underlying encryption scheme and the geometric chart-change assumption discussed above, the noise-flooding replacement contributes statistical distance at most
    \[
        2\sqrt{\frac{q\epsilon_{\mathsf{nf}}}{2}}.
    \]
    For all possible adversaries $\cA$ we have
    \[
        \TVD(\llbracket G_L\rrbracket,\llbracket G_R\rrbracket)
        \leq \mathsf{Adv}_{\mathsf{IND\text{-}CPA}}(\mathcal{B})
            +2\sqrt{\frac{q\epsilon_{\mathsf{nf}}}{2}},
    \]
    for the reduction adversary $\mathcal{B}$ constructed from $\cA$.
\end{theorem}
Notice that in \Cref{alg:indcpa-games}, the attacker is indeed already factored into $q$ subroutines as explained previously in \Cref{sec:query-bound}.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{The Hybrids}
\label{alg:indcpa-games}
Ingredients:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Homomorphic encryption scheme $S=(kg, enc, eval, dec)$ with key types $PK, SK, EVK$ and message type $M$ and ciphertext type $C$.
    \item Query upper bound $q\in \bbN$
\end{itemize}
Global memory:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Keys $pk, evk, sk$
    \item Message ID: $b\in\set{0, 1}$
    \item Adversary query history: $D\in (M \times M \times C)^*$
    \item Number of decryption queries so far $d$
\end{itemize}
Adversary restrictions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The attacker main routine $\cA\Call{.run}{}$ cannot read $b$, $D$, or $sk$.
    $\cA$ can access $pk$ and $evk$, as well as any other memory locations.
    \item The attacker can call the subroutines OEnc, OEval given in \Cref{alg:indcpa-subroutines} for any number of times.
    \item The adversary makes at most $q$ queries to the $ODec$ decryption oracle, enforced in the formal games by a decryption-query counter.
    \begin{itemize}
        \item
        In the game $G_L$, ODec invokes ODecL.
        \item
        In the game $G_R$, ODec invokes ODecR.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
Games $G_L$ and $G_R$:
\begin{algorithmic}
    \Procedure{Game}{}
    \State $(pk, evk, sk) \gets kg$
    \State $b\gets \set{0, 1}$
    \For{$t\in\set{1, \ldots, q}$}
    \State $i\gets \cA^{OEnc, OEval}\Call{.DecQuery}{t}$
    \State $y_i\gets ODec(i)$
    \EndFor
    \State $b^*\gets\cA^{OEnc, OEval}\Call{.guess}{}$
    \State $win\gets b^* = b$
    \State \Return $win$
    \EndProcedure
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Oracles}
\label{alg:indcpa-subroutines}
Same ingredient and shares global memory with the top-level \Cref{alg:indcpa-games}.

Subroutines:
\begin{algorithmic}
    \Procedure{OEnc}{$m_0, m_1$}
        \State $c\gets enc(pk, m_b)$
        \State $D\gets D \doubleplus (m_0, m_1, c)$
        \State\Return $c$
    \EndProcedure
    \State
    \Procedure{OEval}{$f, \cI$}
        \State $m_0'\gets f(m_{\cI, 0})$
        \State $m_1'\gets f(m_{\cI, 1})$
        \State $c'\gets Eval(f, c_{\cI})$
        \State $D\gets D \doubleplus (m_0', m_1', c')$
        \State \Return $c'$
    \EndProcedure
    \State
    \Procedure{ODecL}{$i$}
        \State $y\gets Dec(D_i(c))$
        \State $y'\gets\DG{y}{\sigma}$
        \State \Return $y'$
    \EndProcedure
    \State
    \Procedure{ODecR}{$i$}
        \State $y\gets D_i(m_b)$
        \State $y'\gets\DG{y}{\sigma}$
    \State\Return $y'$
    \EndProcedure
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Proof Overview}

We now present a top-down sketch for the main steps in the security proof of \Cref{thm:noise-flooding-secure}.
The current formal proof follows the following hybrid sequence.

\paragraph{Step 1: split the real game.}
The real IND-CPAD game is first presented as open challenger/adversary code linked with the real IND-CPAD oracle.
This is an exact program equality:
the closed game and the linked presentation have the same semantics.
The point of the presentation change is that the trace compiler can inspect the open code and compile calls to the selected decryption identifier.

\paragraph{Step 2: compile the first $q$ decrypt calls.}
Applying the trace compiler to the open game produces a program in which the first $q$ calls to $ODec$ are exposed as explicit call sites.
The real oracle and the simulator oracle both maintain the same decryption counter; after $q$ successful decrypt calls, further decrypt attempts assert-fail on both sides.
Thus replacing the first $q$ selected calls covers all non-failing decryption responses.

\paragraph{Step 3: prove the one-call decrypt replacement.}
Let $\Phi$ be the heap invariant saying that the challenge keys are either fully uninitialized or fully initialized, that initialized keys are valid, and that every table row is a valid ciphertext for the selected challenge plaintext.
The real and simulated decrypt bodies share a deterministic prefix:
they read and increment the decrypt counter, read the table, and branch on whether the two stored plaintext labels are equal.
This prefix is proved by ordinary Hoare reasoning to preserve $\Phi$ and to extract a certified table row for the continuation.

If the two labels are unequal, both decrypt continuations return \texttt{None}, so the Pythagorean cost is zero.
If the labels are equal, the real continuation samples a flooded decryption around $Dec(sk,c)$, while the simulated continuation samples a flooded value around the stored plaintext.
Approximate correctness and the table invariant give that these centers are within the recorded error bound.
The discrete-Gaussian KL calculation then gives a one-call Pythagorean cost
\[
    \epsilon_{\mathsf{nf}}=\frac{n}{2\gamma^2}.
\]
Formally, the deterministic prefix contributes a leading zero in the tuple of Pythagorean costs, so the checked one-call judgment has loss shape $[0;\epsilon_{\mathsf{nf}}]$.
The final-marginal theorem simplifies this to additive error $\sqrt{\epsilon_{\mathsf{nf}}/2}$ for one call.

\paragraph{Step 4: lift one call to $q$ compiled calls.}
The trace-compiler theorem composes the one-call replacement over the first $q$ selected decrypt calls.
By Pythagorean preservation and \LabTirName{Micciancio-Walter}, this gives an additive error
\[
    \sqrt{\frac{q\epsilon_{\mathsf{nf}}}{2}}
\]
between the compiled real game and the compiled decrypt-hybrid game.
The security theorem is stated with twice this compile contribution because the final bound is phrased in terms of the winning advantage rather than just the one-sided completed-output distance.

\paragraph{Step 5: identify the decrypt hybrid with an IND-CPA reduction.}
The decrypt-hybrid oracle keeps the real encrypt/eval behavior but replaces decryption by the simulator response.
The remaining game-identification work is to rewrite this closed sim-decrypt game into the existing IND-CPA reduction presentation:
the reduction uses the IND-CPA encryption oracle for challenge encryptions, stores the same table information, answers decrypt queries by table plaintext plus flooding noise, and finally forwards the adversary's guess.
This identification should be understood at the level of the returned winning bit; the two presentations may use different internal heap locations for bookkeeping.
The Coq development reflects this distinction with a value-only postcondition, \texttt{same\_game\_result\_opt}, for the final endpoint bridge.
Once these exact or zero-error identifications are composed with the nonzero compile step, IND-CPA security of the underlying encryption scheme bounds the final endpoint.

The formal hybrids used in the Coq statement are:
\[
\begin{array}{rcl}
H_0 &=& \texttt{ind\_cpad\_game\_code},\\
H_1 &=& \texttt{ind\_cpad\_compiled\_sim\_decrypt\_game\_code},\\
H_2 &=& \texttt{ind\_cpad\_compiled\_sim\_decrypt\_self\_link\_game\_code},\\
H_3 &=& \texttt{ind\_cpad\_sim\_decrypt\_game\_code},\\
H_4' &=& \texttt{ind\_cpa\_reduction\_linked\_game\_code},\\
H_4 &=& \texttt{ind\_cpa\_reduction\_game\_code}.
\end{array}
\]
In $H_1$, compiled decrypt calls use the simulator oracle, while residual uncompiled calls are still linked to the real oracle.
In $H_2$, the same compiled code is instead linked entirely with the simulator oracle.
The intended distance bounds are
\[
  H_0 \xrightarrow{\sqrt{q\epsilon_{\mathsf{nf}}/2}} H_1
  \xrightarrow{0} H_2
  \xrightarrow{0} H_3
  \xrightarrow{0} H_4'
  \xrightarrow{0} H_4 .
\]
The first arrow is the compile-rule replacement with the invariant-compatible moved-guess factoring.
The bridge $H_1\to H_2$ is the bounded-query endpoint: after the first $q$ selected decrypt calls have been compiled, the decryption counter guarantees that any further decrypt call assert-fails whether it is linked to the real oracle or to the simulator oracle.
The bridge $H_2\to H_3$ is the ordinary compiler correctness theorem, because the compiled code is linked with the same simulator package that supplied the compiled calls.
Both of these bridges should preserve the full completed output, including the heap.
The bridge $H_3\to H_4'$ is the substantive endpoint identification: it compares the simulated-decrypt IND-CPAD presentation with the IND-CPA reduction simulator while ignoring bookkeeping heap differences.
This bridge is exact only at the returned-bit level, which is sufficient because the subsequent advantage bound projects the completed game distribution to its Boolean result.
The final bridge $H_4'\to H_4$ is ordinary package-linking correctness for the outer IND-CPA encryption oracle and preserves the full completed output.

Concretely, the $H_3\to H_4'$ bridge uses the same initial samples in both games:
the challenge bit $b$ and key triple $(pk,evk,sk)$ are drawn once and coupled identically.
The IND-CPAD sim-decrypt presentation stores $b,pk,evk,sk$ in its challenger heap and gives $(pk,evk)$ directly to the adversary.
The linked IND-CPA reduction stores $b,pk,evk$ in the outer IND-CPA oracle heap, stores readiness plus $(pk,evk)$ in the simulator heap, and then gives the same $(pk,evk)$ to the same adversary.
The table and decrypt counter are shared logical state: encrypt and evaluation calls append the same plaintext pair/ciphertext rows, while decrypt calls consult the same row index and return either \texttt{None} for unequal plaintext labels or the same flooded plaintext distribution for equal labels.
Thus the maintained invariant for the endpoint proof should not be literal heap equality.
It should relate the two heaps by equality of the adversary-visible table, decrypt counter, public keys, evaluation key, and hidden challenge bit, with the IND-CPA oracle heap carrying the encryption secret state on the reduction side and the IND-CPAD challenger heap carrying the secret key on the sim-decrypt side.
Under this relation every adversary call receives the same answer distribution, and the final returned value is the same predicate $b'=b$.

This last projection requires a small probabilistic gluing argument.
Suppose the result marginals of two completed output distributions are within total variation $\varepsilon$.
First take a maximal coupling of these projected Boolean-result marginals, so that the two projected results agree with probability at least $1-\varepsilon$.
Then disintegrate each full completed-output distribution over its result fiber and sample full outputs conditionally from the two fibers selected by the projected coupling.
The resulting lifted coupling has the original full distributions as marginals.
Moreover, whenever the projected coupling selected equal results, both conditional samples lie in the same result fiber, so the lifted full outputs satisfy the result-level postcondition.
Thus a TVD bound on projected results can be converted back into an additive-error coupling over full completed outputs with a value-only postcondition.
This is the formal role of \texttt{projected\_total\_variation\_coupling}; it is used to prove the result-level triangle rule for composing the final exact bridge with the previous full-output bridges.

The formal development currently proves Steps 1--4 for the first decrypt-replacement hybrid, modulo the chart-change axiom for nearby local Gaussian charts, and the final additive-error theorem is now assembled from named intermediate bridges.
The remaining formal Step~5 obligations are the bounded-query identification from the mixed-link compiled sim-decrypt game to the self-linked sim-decrypt game, and the returned-bit identification from the closed sim-decrypt game to the linked IND-CPA reduction presentation.
