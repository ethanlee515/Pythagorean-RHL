\begin{abstract}
Noise flooding is a standard defense against decryption attacks on approximate
homomorphic encryption, but its security proof is unusually sensitive to
composition.  Replacing each of \(q\) adaptive decryption answers with a
statistically close simulation and applying an ordinary hybrid argument loses
linearly in \(q\).  The cryptographic proof instead accumulates conditional
Kullback--Leibler (KL) costs and converts to statistical distance once, giving
the parameter-critical square-root loss.

We give a machine-checked, game-based account of this argument in Rocq and
SSProve.  For an abstract approximate-FHE scheme satisfying explicit metric,
support-correctness, and IND-CPA assumptions, the development constructs an
IND-CPA reduction for every \(q\)-query IND-CPAD adversary and proves

\[
 \Pr[\mathsf{IND\text{-}CPAD}_{\mathsf{NF}}^{\mathcal A}=1]
 \leq \beta_{\mathsf{CPA}}(\mathcal B_{\mathcal A,q})
       + \frac{\sqrt{qn}}{2\gamma}.
\]

Here \(n\) is the plaintext-chart dimension and \(\gamma\) is the flooding-width
multiplier.  The proof extends SSProve with countable discrete-Gaussian
analysis, completed-output quantitative judgments, and a trace compiler that
lifts one-call KL bounds to adaptive oracle programs.  The checked theorem
isolates the good-execution reduction: imperfect correctness can be added by
one standard up-to-bad layer once an aggregate failure bound is supplied.
As in SSProve, polynomial-time preservation remains the conventional
metatheoretic audit outside the program semantics.
\end{abstract}
